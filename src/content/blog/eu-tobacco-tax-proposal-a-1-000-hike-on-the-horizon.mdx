On July 16, 2025, the European Commission unveiled the most aggressive revision of the **EU tobacco tax** framework since the directive was first introduced in 1995. The proposal — formally the revised Tobacco Excise Directive — would raise minimum excise rates on cigarettes, rolling tobacco, and novel nicotine products across all 27 member states. It would also raise the minimum tax on premium cigars and cigarillos by up to **1,100%** in some markets. That's not a typo. For a country currently taxing premium Habanos at the minimum permitted level, the floor would jump roughly twelve-fold.

If this passes in its current form, the European cigar market as we know it ends. The question is whether it will pass.

## What's actually in the proposal

The headline numbers are dramatic, but the structure matters more than the percentages. The directive sets *minimum* tax rates that all 27 member states must apply. Countries can tax higher — and many already do — but no country can go lower. The current minimums are decades old and lag inflation badly. The Commission's proposal updates them sharply upward across four categories:

- **Cigarettes:** minimum excise up to **139%** of current floor
- **Rolling tobacco:** minimum excise up to **258%** of current floor
- **Cigars and cigarillos:** up to **1,100%** in markets currently at the floor
- **Novel products** (heated tobacco, vapes, nicotine pouches): brought under EU minimums for the first time

The proposal also tightens regulation on raw tobacco trading — aimed at illicit manufacturers but with knock-on effects for small premium cigar importers — and bundles in a [coordinated approach to enforcement](https://taxation-customs.ec.europa.eu/excise-duty/excise-duty-tobacco_en) across member states.

The Commission projects **€15 billion** in new annual tax revenue and **€6 billion** in healthcare savings. Whether those numbers hold up depends on whether higher prices reduce consumption (the public health bet) or drive smokers to the black market (the industry warning).

## Why cigars get hit hardest

The 1,100% number for premium cigars is the headline, and it deserves a closer look. The math: most EU countries currently tax cigars below the cigarette equivalent, on the historical reasoning that cigars are luxury goods consumed in much smaller volumes than cigarettes and therefore less of a public health priority. That framing was preserved across every revision of the Tobacco Excise Directive since 1995.

This proposal changes the framing. It treats cigars and cigarettes as equivalent products for tax-floor purposes — meaning the minimum tax per stick rises to a level comparable to a cigarette pack. For markets like Sweden, Portugal, and Italy, where the historical cigar tax was minimal, the percentage increase is enormous even though the absolute price impact on a $20 premium Habano is "only" a few extra euros per stick.

For [La Casa del Habano franchises](/blog/la-casa-del-habano-the-global-embassy-of-cuban-cigars/) and specialist retailers in those markets, that math is brutal. A box of 25 Habanos that currently retails at €400 could rise to €500-700 depending on national implementation. Demand at that price point isn't elastic enough to maintain volume.

## Who's opposing it (and who's pushing it)

The directive requires **unanimous approval** from all 27 member states. That's the bar. Any one country can block it. The opposition has organized faster than the Commission expected:

- **Sweden** is the most vocal opponent. The country built its public health success around *snus* (a smokeless tobacco product) and treats the proposed framework as a direct attack on the national harm-reduction strategy. The Swedish Tobacco Association has explicitly called the directive an existential threat.
- **Italy** opposes on small-producer grounds. Italy's cigar tradition includes regional artisanal manufacturers (notably Toscano), and the proposal disproportionately burdens niche premium production over mass-market cigarettes.
- **Greece, Portugal, and Bulgaria** have raised concerns about national tax sovereignty.
- **Germany and France** have signaled openness in principle but want substantial revisions to the cigar treatment specifically.

The strongest push for the directive comes from the **European Cancer Plan** coalition and northern European public-health blocs, which see harmonized tobacco taxation as the most effective tool for reducing consumption across the continent. Industry pushback, including from the **European Cigar Manufacturers Association (ECMA)**, has called the proposal "out of touch and irresponsible" and is lobbying aggressively at the member-state level.

## What happens next

Three scenarios are realistic. The first: the directive passes substantially intact. Sweden, Italy, or another opposing state caves under pressure or trades a vote for concessions on another EU matter. European cigar prices rise 30-60% by 2028.

The second: the directive passes but with carve-outs for premium cigars. This is the [industry's preferred outcome](https://www.ecma.eu) — preserving cigarette and rolling-tobacco hikes while keeping the cigar floor near current levels. It would acknowledge that premium cigars represent a small fraction of EU tobacco consumption (under 1% by volume) and aren't a meaningful public health vector.

The third: the directive stalls. Sweden's opposition holds, the unanimous-approval requirement kills the bill, and the Commission has to re-table a watered-down version. This is what happened in 2014 with an earlier attempt at harmonization. Recent precedent suggests it could happen again.

The proposed transition period is **four years**, with full implementation by **2028** if approved. That's the window European aficionados need to think in.

## What European aficionados should do now

If you're in the EU and smoke premium cigars regularly, three concrete moves make sense in 2026:

- **Stock your humidor.** Prices in the proposed implementation window will likely rise even before the directive formally passes — supply-chain participants are already pricing in regulatory risk. A box of 25 Habanos bought now at €400 will likely save you €100-200 by 2028. See our guide to [proper cigar storage](/blog/the-silent-keeper-why-proper-cigar-storage-is-your-secret-to-unforgettable-smokes/) for the humidor setup that makes long-term aging viable.
- **Document your provenance.** If anti-illicit-trade enforcement tightens as part of this directive, casual cross-border cigar carrying (which is currently tolerated in personal-use quantities) may face stricter inspection. Keep receipts for high-value purchases.
- **Pay attention to national implementation.** Even if the directive passes, member states have latitude in how they implement. Sweden, Italy, and a few others may keep cigar tax floors near current levels for as long as possible. Your specific cost depends on your country's choice within the directive's framework.

The broader [post-Imperial Brands ownership shift at Habanos S.A.](/blog/habanos-sa-unpacking-the-landmark-ownership-change/) is already pushing premium cigar prices upward independently of any EU tax change. The combined effect of those two pressures — corporate repositioning toward premium pricing plus regulatory tax floors — means the cheap European Habano era is ending whether or not this specific directive passes.

The European cigar market built up over the last 30 years was shaped by relatively forgiving tobacco-tax floors. This directive proposes to end that era. Whether it succeeds depends on a handful of national vetoes — and on whether aficionados across the continent organize fast enough to make their member states think twice.
